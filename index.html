About Me

I am a Postdoctoral Research Fellow at the Epilepsy Center, Neurological Institute, Cleveland Clinic, working at the intersection of neuroinformatics, signal processing, and neuroscience. My research focuses on developing quantitative, biomarker-driven methods to analyze intracranial brain signals (sEEG) for epilepsy.

I received my Ph.D. in Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning from the Tokyo University of Agriculture and Technology, where my research focused on biosignal processing and the development of machine learning algorithms for brain–computer interface (BCI) systems. My work integrated signal processing, feature extraction, and statistical learning methods to analyze neurophysiological data and build robust predictive models.

Prior to my current role, I served as a Research Assistant Professor in Machine Learning Engineering at the Tokyo University of Agriculture and Technology, Japan, and as an Assistant Professor in the Department of Computer Science and Engineering at Pabna University of Science and Technology, Bangladesh. In these roles, I taught courses in signal processing, machine learning, and computer science, while developing AI-driven methods for analyzing biomedical signals and supporting clinical decision-making.

Research Focus

By integrating band-specific and slow-timescale coupling analyses with advanced signal processing and data-driven methods, I identify quantitative biomarkers that distinguish epileptogenic from non-epileptogenic tissue and support surgical planning.

Using source-space connectivity, graph theory, and advanced signal processing, I characterize pathological mesoscale topology and develop quantitative network biomarkers for epileptogenic zone prediction and surgical planning.
